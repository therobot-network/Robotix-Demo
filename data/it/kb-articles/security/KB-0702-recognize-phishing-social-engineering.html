How to Recognize Phishing and Social Engineering

Summary

Phishing and social engineering attacks try to trick you into handing over credentials, money, or sensitive information by pretending to be someone or something you trust. This article describes common attack types and gives you practical techniques to spot them before you click.

Why This Matters

Over 90% of successful cyberattacks begin with a phishing email or social engineering attempt. Attackers targeting Robotix IT may impersonate Microsoft, Okta, HR, IT, senior leadership, vendors, or colleagues. Recognizing the warning signs is your most effective defense — no technology can fully replace your judgment.

Common Attack Types

Email Phishing

Mass emails sent to many recipients, often impersonating well-known brands (Microsoft, DocuSign, FedEx, Amazon) or internal departments (IT, HR, Payroll).

Spear Phishing

Targeted emails addressed to you by name, referencing real details about your role, projects, or colleagues. These are harder to spot but use the same warning signs below.

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

A phone call from someone claiming to be IT support, your bank, a vendor, or even a Robotix IT executive, asking you to provide credentials, approve an MFA push, or transfer funds.

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

A text message with a link or request, often claiming your account is locked or a package could not be delivered.

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

An attacker impersonates an executive or vendor via email to request urgent wire transfers, gift card purchases, or changes to payroll direct deposit.

Steps: How to Evaluate a Suspicious Message

1. Check the sender address — not just the display name

  • Click or tap the sender's name to reveal the actual email address.
  • Legitimate Robotix IT emails come from @robotix.com. Microsoft 365 alerts come from @microsoft.com. Okta notifications come from @okta.com.
  • Watch for subtle misspellings: @summ1tit.com, @robotix-services.com, @microsoft-support.com.

2. Look for urgency and pressure tactics

Phrases like "Immediate action required," "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours," "Respond before end of day," or "Do not share this with anyone" are designed to stop you from thinking critically. Slow down.

3. Hover over links before clicking

  • On desktop: hover your mouse over any link and look at the URL shown in the browser's status bar or tooltip.
  • The domain in the URL must match the company it claims to be from.
  • https://login.robotix.com.phishsite.ru/okta is not a Robotix IT URL — the real domain is phishsite.ru.
  • When in doubt, navigate to the website directly by typing the address into your browser rather than clicking the link.

4. Watch for unexpected attachments

  • Robotix IT will not email you unexpected .zip, .exe, .iso, .js, or macro-enabled Office files (.xlsm, .docm).
  • Even PDF files can contain malicious links.
  • If you did not request the document, verify with the sender using a separate channel (Teams message or phone call) before opening it.

5. Verify unusual requests through a second channel

  • If an email from your manager or an executive asks you to buy gift cards, change a bank account, or approve an urgent payment, call or Teams-message the person directly using the contact details already in your directory — not a number or link provided in the email.
  • The CEO of Robotix IT will never ask you to buy Google Play cards.

6. Be cautious on phone calls

  • Robotix IT support will never call you unsolicited and ask for your password or ask you to approve an Okta push notification you did not initiate.
  • If a caller claims to be from IT and asks for your credentials, hang up and call the Service Desk directly at +1 (800) 555-7866 to verify.

7. Apply the SLAM checklist

Letter Check
S — Sender Is the sender's actual address legitimate?
L — Links Do hover-revealed URLs match the claimed organization?
A — Attachments Were attachments expected? Is the file type safe?
M — Message Does the tone, urgency, or request feel unusual?

If any SLAM check fails, do not click — report it.

Warning Signs Summary

  • Unexpected password reset or MFA prompt you did not initiate
  • Request to "verify your account" by clicking a link
  • Invoice or payment request you were not expecting
  • Executive asking for an urgent action outside normal process
  • Grammar errors, odd phrasing, or mismatched logos
  • Link URL does not match the claimed sender's domain
  • Caller knows your name but cannot verify their own identity through official channels
