Set Up a Reliable Home Network for Remote Work

Summary

A reliable home network is the foundation for productive remote work. Dropped video calls, GlobalProtect VPN disconnects, and slow file transfers are often caused by a weak router, an overloaded home network, or a poor Wi-Fi placement — not by Summit IT's infrastructure. This article provides practical guidance for setting up and optimizing your home network so you can work remotely without connectivity interruptions. Summit IT does not provide or support home networking hardware, but these best practices significantly reduce help desk tickets.

Before You Begin

  • You will need GlobalProtect VPN installed and working to access internal Summit IT resources from home (see KB-0302 for Windows, KB-0303 for Mac).
  • Run a speed test at https://speed.cloudflare.com to establish your current home internet baseline. For comfortable remote work including video calls, you need at least:
  • 25 Mbps download / 10 Mbps upload for one remote worker
  • 50 Mbps download / 20 Mbps upload if other household members are also streaming or gaming
  • Note whether you are using Wi-Fi or a wired Ethernet connection from your router to your laptop.

Recommendations

1 — Use a wired Ethernet connection when possible

A wired connection is always more reliable and faster than Wi-Fi, especially for VPN traffic.

  1. Connect an Ethernet cable from your laptop (or your Dell WD19 dock, if you have one) directly to a LAN port on your home router.
  2. Disable Wi-Fi on your laptop while the Ethernet cable is plugged in to avoid traffic routing over both interfaces simultaneously. - Windows: Click the Wi-Fi icon in the taskbar > click Summit-Corp > Disconnect, or toggle Wi-Fi off in Settings > Network & internet. - macOS: Click the Wi-Fi menu bar icon > Turn Wi-Fi Off.
  3. Run the speed test again — wired speeds should be noticeably higher and more consistent.

If your router is in a different room from your workspace, consider a powerline adapter or MoCA adapter to run a wired connection through your home's electrical wiring without drilling walls.

2 — Position your router or Wi-Fi access point for best coverage

If wired is not possible, maximize your Wi-Fi signal quality.

  • Place your router or primary Wi-Fi access point in a central, elevated location — not on the floor, inside a cabinet, or in a corner behind a TV.
  • Keep the router away from microwave ovens, cordless phones, and baby monitors, which cause 2.4 GHz interference.
  • Line of sight between your laptop and the router yields the best performance. Reduce the number of walls between you and the router.
  • If your workspace is more than 30 feet from the router or separated by multiple walls, consider a Wi-Fi extender or, ideally, a mesh Wi-Fi system (e.g., Eero, Google Nest Wi-Fi, TP-Link Deco).

3 — Connect to the 5 GHz band instead of 2.4 GHz

Most modern routers broadcast two bands: 2.4 GHz (longer range, slower, more congested) and 5 GHz (shorter range, much faster, less congested). For a laptop that is reasonably close to the router, 5 GHz is preferred.

  • Many routers use the same SSID for both bands and switch automatically.
  • If your router broadcasts separate SSIDs (e.g., "HomeNetwork" and "HomeNetwork_5G"), connect your work laptop to the 5 GHz network.
  • Check which band you are connected to:
  • Windows: Settings > Network & internet > Wi-Fi > click your network > Properties > look for "Band" (5 GHz or 2.4 GHz).
  • macOS: Hold Option and click the Wi-Fi menu bar icon — the current band is shown in the dropdown.

4 — Restart your router regularly

Home routers can develop memory leaks and stale connection tables over time. A weekly restart keeps them performing well.

  1. Unplug your router's power cable.
  2. Wait 30 seconds.
  3. Plug the power cable back in.
  4. Wait 2 minutes for the router to fully reconnect to your ISP before testing.

Tip: Set a calendar reminder to restart your router every Sunday evening before the work week begins.

5 — Reduce bandwidth competition during work hours

Other devices and household members can saturate your connection during work hours.

  • Pause large downloads (game updates, OS updates, streaming 4K video) on household devices during your working hours.
  • If household members are streaming video during your video calls, ask them to use 1080p (HD) rather than 4K — 4K streaming uses 3–4x more bandwidth.
  • If you have a smart home with many IoT devices (cameras, thermostats, doorbells), consider putting them on a separate guest network on your router to isolate that traffic.

6 — Optimize your laptop's network adapter settings

Windows 11 — Disable power management on the Wi-Fi adapter: 1. Open Device Manager (right-click Start > Device Manager). 2. Expand Network adapters. 3. Right-click your Wi-Fi adapter > Properties > Power Management tab. 4. Uncheck Allow the computer to turn off this device to save power. 5. Click OK.

This prevents Windows from throttling or disconnecting the Wi-Fi adapter when running on battery, which can cause GlobalProtect to drop.

Windows 11 — Set the adapter to maximum performance: 1. In Device Manager, right-click your Wi-Fi adapter > Properties > Advanced tab. 2. Set Wireless Mode to the highest available (e.g., 802.11ax / Wi-Fi 6 if supported). 3. Set Roaming Aggressiveness to Low to prevent the adapter from constantly hunting for a stronger AP.

7 — Keep GlobalProtect connected at all times when working

GlobalProtect is configured to reconnect automatically, but if your connection drops and comes back, the VPN may need a nudge.

  • If you notice internal sites becoming unreachable, click the GlobalProtect globe icon and check whether it shows "Connected."
  • If it shows "Reconnecting" for more than 30 seconds, click Disconnect then Connect and approve the Okta Verify push.
  • For persistent VPN disconnects at home, see KB-0304.

8 — Test your connection and baseline regularly

Keeping a record of your home speeds helps identify when your ISP degrades service.

  1. Run https://speed.cloudflare.com at 8:00 AM, 12:00 PM, and 5:00 PM on a normal workday.
  2. If speeds are consistently below 10 Mbps download or 5 Mbps upload during business hours, contact your ISP to report service degradation.
  3. Share your speed test results with the Service Desk if you open a remote connectivity ticket — this data helps L3 engineers rule out Summit IT infrastructure as the cause.
