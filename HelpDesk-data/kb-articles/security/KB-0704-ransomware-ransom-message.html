Ransomware: What to Do If You See a Ransom Message

Summary

If your screen shows a message demanding payment to unlock your files, you are likely experiencing a ransomware attack. Do not pay. Follow the steps in this article immediately — speed directly affects how much data can be recovered and how far the attack spreads.

Why This Matters

Ransomware encrypts files on your device and any network shares your device can reach, then demands payment for a decryption key. Summit IT maintains secure backups and has an incident response plan — paying the ransom is not necessary and is not approved under any circumstances. Your most important job is to act within the first few minutes to limit the blast radius.

Before You Begin

Ransomware moves fast. Read through all the steps below once, then act immediately. Do not pause to research the ransomware name or try to find a free decryptor — that comes later, handled by the SOC.

Steps

Step 1 — Do not pay the ransom

Summit IT does not authorize ransomware payments. There is no scenario in which paying is the correct first step. Paying does not guarantee file recovery and directly funds criminal organizations.

Step 2 — Disconnect from the network immediately

This is the single most impactful thing you can do to limit spread:

  1. Wi-Fi: Turn off Wi-Fi immediately from your taskbar (Windows) or menu bar (macOS).
  2. Ethernet/dock: Unplug the network cable from your laptop or docking station.
  3. Do not log out, shut down, or restart the device. The SOC needs the device in its current state for forensics. Exception: if the screen is locked by the ransom message and you cannot disconnect from the network in any other way, a forced power-off is acceptable as a last resort.

Step 3 — Call the SOC immediately — this is a P1 emergency

  1. Call the Summit Service Desk now: +1 (800) 555-7866. The service desk is staffed 24/7 for P1 incidents. Say: "I have a ransomware message on my screen."
  2. If you cannot call from your infected device, use your mobile phone or a colleague's computer.
  3. Do not use email from the infected machine.

Step 4 — Photograph the ransom message

Using your mobile phone, take a clear photo of: - The ransom message screen - Any Bitcoin/cryptocurrency wallet addresses shown - Any email addresses or website URLs in the message - The name of the ransomware variant if displayed (e.g., "LockBit," "ALPHV," "Akira")

Send these photos to soc@summitit.com from your phone.

Step 5 — Notify nearby colleagues

If you are in an office, alert colleagues around you that there may be an active incident. Ask them to:

  1. Save any open work immediately.
  2. Disconnect their own devices from the network as a precaution if the SOC advises it.
  3. Do not open any shared drives until the SOC gives the all-clear.

Step 6 — Do not attempt self-remediation

  • Do not run CrowdStrike scans, Windows Defender, or any other tool yourself.
  • Do not delete encrypted files.
  • Do not attempt to restore from backup without SOC direction — backup systems may also be targeted.
  • Do not reformat or reinstall the operating system.

The SOC and the L2/L3 teams will handle all recovery steps.

Step 7 — Preserve your account and change passwords from a clean device

If ransomware is present, attackers may also have your credentials:

  1. From your phone or another unaffected device, change your Okta password at https://summitit.okta.com.
  2. Tell the SOC which applications you were logged into on the infected device.

Troubleshooting / Warning Signs

  • Files have strange extensions or cannot be opened: Encryption may have already started even without a visible ransom note. Disconnect from the network and call the SOC immediately.
  • OneDrive or SharePoint is syncing a large number of renamed files: Ransomware can encrypt cloud-synced files. Pause OneDrive sync immediately (right-click the OneDrive icon in the taskbar and select Pause syncing) and call the SOC.
  • The ransom note says the attack is from a law enforcement agency: This is always a scam. No law enforcement agency encrypts your files and demands Bitcoin. Treat it as ransomware.
  • I think I have a ransom message but I'm not sure: If in doubt, call the SOC. It is always better to escalate a false alarm than to delay response on a real incident.
